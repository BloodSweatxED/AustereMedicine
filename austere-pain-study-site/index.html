Foundation

One pain problem repeats across four hostile settings. The constraint changes. The logic does not.

What "austere" means here

Definition. An austere environment is any place where normal hospital or EMS resources are unavailable and definitive care is hard or slow to reach. The 2024 Wilderness Medical Society guideline chose "austere" rather than "remote" because distance is not the only problem. Weather, terrain, altitude, event volume, infrastructure loss, and scarcity can all sever access to care.

Four shared features

  • Limited access to the patient and limited reassessment during transport.
  • Environmental threat from cold, heat, sun, weather, terrain, altitude, or system failure.
  • Little or no monitoring of oxygen saturation, blood pressure, or ventilation.
  • Limited ability to rescue an adverse drug effect such as stopped breathing.

The four binding constraints

  • Wilderness: distance and self sufficiency.
  • Altitude: distance plus hostile physiology.
  • Mass gatherings: volume and protection of the local health system.
  • Disaster and mass casualty: scarcity so severe that the goal changes.

Core frame

Austere pain care is not "hospital pain care with fewer toys." It is the same pain problem under a different resource constraint. A drug that is safe beside a monitor and ventilator may be dangerous when no one can watch or rescue the patient.

Framework Primer KBFink et al 2024

An austere environment is any place where the resources of a hospital or normal EMS system are not available, and where definitive care is hard or slow to reach. The 2024 Wilderness Medical Society guideline uses the word "austere" on purpose. A patient does not need to be far away to be cut off from care. Bad weather, hard terrain, high altitude, a crowded event, or a damaged local system can make care unavailable even when the patient is close to a hospital.

These settings share four features. The provider has limited access to the patient and limited ability to reassess during transport. The environment threatens the patient through cold, heat, sun, altitude, weather, or system failure. Monitoring is poor or absent, so the provider cannot watch oxygen levels or blood pressure closely. Rescue options are limited if treatment causes harm. A drug that is safe beside a monitor and a ventilator is not the same drug when no one can watch the patient breathe.

The four settings in this chapter differ by the constraint that removes normal care. Wilderness care is limited by distance and self sufficiency. Altitude care adds hostile physiology that changes breathing and drug handling. Mass gathering care is limited by patient volume and by the need to protect the local hospital from overload. Disaster care is limited by scarcity so severe that the goal shifts from the best care for one person to the greatest good for the greatest number.

The 2024 shift: simultaneous selection

The older 2014 guideline used a fixed ladder. Start with the lowest risk treatment, wait, then escalate only after it failed. The 2024 update replaced that with simultaneous selection.

  • Choose several treatments at once when the cause and expected severity make severe pain obvious.
  • Base the choice on the cause of pain, the reported and expected severity, and the clinician's experience.
  • Still favor lower risk tools first or alongside higher risk tools when they fit.
  • Do not force the patient through predictable failure before giving effective relief.

Teaching point

The skill is judgment plus frequent reassessment. A minor sprain may need reassurance, splinting, and oral drugs. An open fracture may need a strong analgesic, splinting, and psychological first aid immediately.

Framework Primer KBRussell et al 2014Fink et al 2024

The central change in the 2024 guideline is the move away from a fixed pain ladder. The older 2014 approach asked the provider to start with the lowest risk treatment, wait for it to fail, and then step up. That approach is too slow when the cause of pain already shows that the patient will need stronger relief.

The 2024 guideline recommends simultaneous selection. The provider chooses treatments based on the cause of pain, the pain the patient reports, the pain the provider expects from the injury, and the provider's skill. This does not mean the provider ignores risk. It means the provider uses several tools at the same time when they fit the case. For an open fracture, the provider can give a strong analgesic, apply a splint, and use psychological first aid in the first minutes. The patient should not have to wait for a weak treatment to fail when failure is predictable.

The two rules that rank every treatment

Rule one: central is riskier than peripheral

  • Central drugs act on the brain and spinal cord.
  • Opioids and benzodiazepines can slow breathing and drop blood pressure.
  • Peripheral drugs act at the site of injury or outside the brain.
  • NSAIDs and local anesthetics do not threaten breathing in the same way.

Rule two: injected is riskier than swallowed or topical

  • Intranasal, intramuscular, intravenous, and intraosseous routes peak fast.
  • Fast onset also means fast arrival of a bad effect.
  • Swallowed and topical routes peak more slowly.
  • Slower onset is safer when monitoring and rescue are limited.

Rules are not laws

Crush injury is the cleanest exception. In most austere settings, NSAIDs are a low risk backbone. In crush injury, NSAIDs become dangerous because the kidney is already under attack from rhabdomyolysis.

Two rules rank the risk of each pain treatment. The first rule is that central drugs are riskier than peripheral drugs. Central drugs act on the brain and spinal cord. Opioids and benzodiazepines are the main examples. They can slow or stop breathing, and they can lower blood pressure. Peripheral drugs act at the injury site or outside the brain. The second rule is that injected routes are riskier than swallowed or topical routes. Intranasal, intramuscular, intravenous, and intraosseous drugs can reach peak blood levels quickly. That speed helps pain, but it also makes a bad effect arrive quickly. Swallowed and topical drugs work more slowly. That slower onset gives a wider margin when the provider cannot monitor the patient closely.

These rules guide judgment. They do not replace it. Crush injury is the key exception. NSAIDs are usually a low risk backbone in austere care, but in crush injury they can worsen kidney injury. The provider must know when the usual ranking flips.

Sequence, masking, and pain measurement

Order before analgesia

  1. Scene safety for the patient and rescuers.
  2. Primary survey for airway, breathing, and circulation threats.
  3. Secondary survey to find the cause and severity of pain.
  4. Pain control.

Masking myth

Treating pain does not hide a serious injury or ruin the exam. Do not withhold pain relief because of that outdated fear.

Use pain scales correctly

  • Use a 0 to 10 scale or a simple patient description before and after treatment.
  • The point is to judge whether treatment worked.
  • Do not create a fixed score that forces a fixed drug. Evidence shows threshold rules increase drug related harm.

Framework Primer KBFink et al 2024

Pain control is never the first task. The provider first makes the scene safe for the patient and the rescuers. Then the provider completes the primary survey and treats immediate threats to airway, breathing, and circulation. Next comes the secondary survey, which identifies the cause and severity of pain. Only then does the provider build the pain plan.

One old concern should not guide care. Pain relief does not hide a serious injury or ruin the exam. The provider should not withhold analgesia because of that fear.

The provider should measure pain before and after treatment. A 0 to 10 scale is acceptable, and a simple description from the patient is also useful. The purpose is to decide whether the treatment worked. The scale should not force a specific drug at a specific number. Threshold rules increase drug related harm because they replace judgment with a number.

Wilderness

The binding constraint is distance and self sufficiency. The kit is what the team carried.

Case and clinical picture

Case study: wilderness

A 34 year old hiker is two days from the trailhead. She fell and now has a closed, clearly deformed ankle. She is alert with normal vital signs and no other injury. There is no equipment to start an IV. Pain stops her from bearing weight, so she cannot walk out. The party has a small medical kit.

  • Most wilderness pain is musculoskeletal: strains, sprains, dislocations, and fractures.
  • Other common pain problems include cold injury, bites, stings, and burns.
  • Most problems are minor and respond to non drug care plus non-opioid drugs.
  • The hard cases are the few severe injuries where pain blocks evacuation.

Clinical picture

Do not chase a full diagnostic workup. Identify mechanism, location, quality, severity, deformity, tissue appearance, and whether pain control will permit packaging or movement. Measure severity before and after treatment.

Non drug care

Psychological first aid

  • Pain is shaped by emotional state. Anxiety raises pain. Trust, calm, distraction, and explanations lower it.
  • ABCDE gives a field structure: anchoring, breathing, control and cognitive shift, decrease nociception and distract, explanations and expectations.
  • Give the patient a role when possible, such as holding pressure or helping count breaths.

Splinting and immobilization

  • Stopping painful motion is direct analgesia.
  • Splint early when deformity or suspected fracture is present.

PRICE and MEAT

  • PRICE is the default when fracture is suspected or cannot be ruled out.
  • MEAT is for isolated ligament or tendon injury after fracture is ruled out.
  • Ice or snow can reduce nerve conduction and swelling, but cold exposure is itself a hazard.

Cold therapy warning

Use short cycles, roughly 10 minutes cooling and 10 minutes rewarming, and avoid frostbite or core temperature loss.

Wilderness KBFramework Primer KB

The first wilderness treatment is not a drug. Psychological first aid matters because anxiety raises pain and calm lowers it. The provider anchors the patient, coaches slow breathing, gives the patient a small role, reduces the pain signal with other tools, distracts the patient, and explains each step before it happens. These actions reduce panic and make movement possible.

Splinting is also analgesia. A broken or unstable limb hurts because movement pulls on injured tissue. Immobilization stops that movement. PRICE remains the default for suspected fracture because the provider often cannot rule out a fracture in the field. MEAT can fit a soft tissue injury only after fracture is excluded. Cold therapy may reduce pain and swelling, but in a cold environment the provider must use it carefully. Short cycles and a barrier protect the skin and the patient's core temperature.

The oral backbone

  • Acetaminophen. Pain and fever control. Not anti inflammatory. Adult healthy ceiling is 4 g/day or 60 mg/kg in four divided doses. Use care with liver disease or heavy alcohol use. Oral route preferred if the patient can swallow. Rectal route works about as well when needed.
  • NSAIDs. Ibuprofen and naproxen are common. Meloxicam suits field use because it is once daily. Ketorolac is injectable when the patient cannot swallow, but injected ketorolac is no better than oral ibuprofen for pain.
  • Ceiling effect. Higher NSAID doses do not add much relief above moderate dosing, such as 400 mg of ibuprofen. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time.
  • Combination. Acetaminophen plus an NSAID beats either alone and often controls traumatic pain without an opioid.

NSAID cautions

Avoid NSAIDs in known kidney problems, pregnancy, prior stomach weight loss surgery, history of GI bleeding, and situations where reduced clotting or dehydration makes harm more likely.

Opioids, no-vein routes, and ketamine

Opioids

  • Use when non drug care and the oral backbone are not enough, or when severe pain is obvious from the start.
  • Main dangers are respiratory depression and loss of airway protection.
  • Anyone giving strong opioids needs basic airway skills. Carry naloxone when using stronger non-oral opioids.
  • Naloxone may trigger sudden withdrawal in a patient with long term opioid use.

Needle free options

  • Intranasal fentanyl: fast, strong, no vein. Use 50 microgram/mL solution. Give 0.2 to 0.3 mL per nostril and split between nostrils. Volumes over 1 mL per nostril are swallowed. Atomizer preferred.
  • Oral transmucosal fentanyl: lozenge absorbed through the mouth lining. Military field use supports it.
  • Sublingual fentanyl and sufentanil: likely safe and effective, but studied mostly with monitoring.
  • Transdermal opioids: too slow for acute pain and not recommended.

Ketamine

  • Pain dose is 0.1 to 0.5 mg/kg IV or 1 to 2 mg/kg IM.
  • Preserves breathing, airway reflexes, and blood pressure better than opioids.
  • Useful when blood loss is a concern.
  • Avoid drifting into the middle range between analgesia and full sedation, where hallucinations, dread, and delirium can occur.

Volumes above 1 milliliter per nostril are swallowed rather than absorbed. An atomizer improves delivery. The oral transmucosal fentanyl lozenge is another needle free option. This makes it useful when blood loss is possible. The provider must stay in the pain dose range. The middle range between pain dosing and full sedation can cause hallucinations, fear, and delirium.

Local and regional anesthesia

  • Topical anesthetics fit wounds, stings, and burns with little systemic absorption.
  • Ophthalmic anesthetic drops can allow a patient with corneal scratch or snow blindness to open the eye and self evacuate. Short repeated use under 72 hours showed no important harm in pooled ED data. Protect the numbed eye from dryness, foreign objects, and sun.
  • Field infiltration uses dilute lidocaine, usually 0.5 to 1%, around a wound. Use a thin needle, inject while withdrawing, and avoid vessels.
  • Field expedient nerve blocks should have reliable landmarks, predictable numbness, low risk anatomy, volumes within safe dose limits, slow absorption, and no special equipment requirement.
  • Portable ultrasound is recommended when equipment and skill are available, but training should be gained in a controlled setting first.
  • After a block, pad and protect the limb, check blood flow, and watch for compartment syndrome that the patient may no longer feel.

Hematoma block dose

For a distal forearm fracture, use plain lidocaine only. Max is 1.5 to 2 mg/kg because fracture blood connects to marrow and absorption can be fast. For most adults, 5 to 10 mL of 1% lidocaine is safe and effective.

Backup local anesthetic

Diphenhydramine 1% can numb a wound when lidocaine is unavailable or allergy exists. It works within about 5 minutes in most patients. It stings more, is weaker and shorter, and can cause mild sedation.

Altitude

The binding constraint is distance plus hostile physiology. The mountain changes breathing, drug handling, and margin for error.

Altitude changes drug handling own it angle

Original contribution angle: altitude pharmacokinetic gap

The KB identifies this as a real chapter opportunity. We know far less than we should about how altitude changes analgesic pharmacokinetics in injured humans.

  • At altitude, lower oxygen and lower pressure can change how the body absorbs, distributes, breaks down, and clears drugs.
  • The clearest KB example is acetaminophen under simulated high altitude. The drug stayed in the body longer, total exposure rose, and clearance slowed.
  • The KB attributes this to reduced activity of a liver enzyme that normally breaks the drug down.
  • Most evidence is animal data or simulation, not field data from injured humans at altitude.
  • Practical stance: dose conservatively, expect stronger or longer effects, and name the gap instead of overstating certainty.

Why opioids are more dangerous at altitude

  1. The climber is already hypoxic because the air contains less usable oxygen.
  2. Survival depends on breathing harder.
  3. Two ventilatory drives matter: response to low oxygen and response to rising carbon dioxide.
  4. Opioids blunt both drives and can cause irregular breathing.
  5. Altitude itself causes periodic breathing during sleep. Opioids can make an unmonitored sleep period dangerous.

Practical rule

Fear opioids more at altitude than at sea level. If an opioid is unavoidable for severe injury, use the lowest dose, choose short acting drug, avoid overnight dosing when possible, and keep naloxone ready.

Opioids are more dangerous at altitude because the patient already has less oxygen available. To compensate, the body must breathe harder. The body uses two main signals to drive that breathing. One responds to low oxygen. The other responds to rising carbon dioxide.

Opioids blunt both signals. They reduce the response to low oxygen and the response to carbon dioxide, and they can make breathing irregular. Altitude also causes periodic breathing during sleep, with cycles of faster breathing, slower breathing, and pauses. An opioid added to this unstable pattern can push an unmonitored patient into dangerous underbreathing.

If a severe injury requires an opioid, the provider should use the lowest dose, choose a short acting drug, avoid overnight dosing when possible, and keep naloxone ready. The provider should tie the decision to the breathing mechanism rather than treat the opioid as a routine field analgesic.

Management choices at altitude

Non-opioid first

  • Acetaminophen and ibuprofen are first choices for high altitude headache.
  • Some ibuprofen studies found it about as effective as acetazolamide for preventing acute mountain sickness, but this does not mean it treats HACE or resolves the illness.
  • NSAIDs need kidney caution because climbers are often dehydrated.

Ketamine and regional techniques

  • Ketamine keeps its austere value because it relieves severe pain without opioid-like respiratory depression and tends to preserve blood pressure.
  • Small series report safe ketamine use in mountain rescue and above 3,900 meters, with no serious complications or drops in oxygen.
  • Local and regional anesthesia are attractive because they avoid sedation and avoid threatening breathing.

Inhaled analgesics

  • Nitrous oxide loses effect as gas pressure falls with altitude.
  • In extreme cold below about minus 5.5 degrees Celsius, nitrous can liquefy and then deliver a dangerously low oxygen mixture as the cylinder empties.
  • Methoxyflurane is in the shared drug reference, but the altitude KB focuses the inhaled warning on nitrous oxide.

Altitude illness drugs

  • Acetazolamide and dexamethasone are altitude illness drugs, not pain drugs.
  • Mention them only to explain why headache evaluation changes pain treatment, then point to altitude illness guidance.

The first pain drugs for high altitude headache are acetaminophen and ibuprofen. Ibuprofen has been studied for prevention of acute mountain sickness and may help prevent mild illness, but it should not be treated as a cure for severe altitude disease. NSAIDs also require kidney caution because climbers are often dehydrated.

Ketamine remains valuable at altitude. It relieves severe pain without suppressing breathing the way opioids do, and it tends to preserve blood pressure. Small series describe ketamine use in mountain rescue and at elevations above 3,900 meters without serious complications or oxygen drops, but the data set is small. Local and regional techniques may be even cleaner for an isolated limb injury because they avoid sedation and do not threaten breathing.

Nitrous oxide fits poorly at altitude. As altitude rises, the pressure of inhaled gas falls and the analgesic effect drops. Extreme cold adds another problem. Below about minus 5.5 degrees Celsius, nitrous can liquefy, and the cylinder can deliver a dangerously low oxygen mixture as it empties. Acetazolamide and dexamethasone belong to altitude illness care rather than pain care. The pain chapter should mention them only to explain why a headache workup changes treatment.

At a mass gathering, the key skill is not choosing the strongest analgesic. It is recognizing when the pain complaint is actually heat illness, intoxication, withdrawal, or another condition where analgesia alone can delay the right treatment.

Disaster and mass casualty KBTCCC sources

The disaster constraint is scarcity. A 30 year old is pulled from a collapsed building after several hours under rubble. His lower leg is crushed. The field hospital has limited drugs, limited monitoring, and many other patients waiting. The team must relieve his pain without harming him and without using resources that other patients need.

In a true mass casualty event, the goal changes. In the hospital, the team usually tries to optimize care for the patient in front of them. In a disaster, the team must pursue the greatest good for the greatest number. The team measures resources in staff, supplies, and structure. Pain relief becomes part of that rationing calculation. A plan that requires one nurse to watch one airway for hours may cost care for several other patients.

Crush injury and the NSAID inversion own it angle

Original contribution angle: crush injury NSAID inversion

The usual austere instinct is to reach for an NSAID. In crush injury, that instinct flips. The kidney is already threatened by rhabdomyolysis, and NSAIDs can reduce renal blood flow and worsen acute kidney injury.

  • Building collapse and earthquake injuries often affect limbs, especially lower limbs.
  • Crush injury damages muscle through pressure and through reperfusion when blood flow returns.
  • Damaged muscle releases contents into blood. Rhabdomyolysis can injure kidneys and cause acute kidney injury.
  • Crush syndrome is the dangerous whole body result and is cited in the KB as the second most common cause of death after a major disaster.
  • Early aggressive fluid resuscitation is the key kidney protective treatment. This is not analgesia, but it shapes analgesia.

Hold the NSAID

In suspected crush injury, do not use the usual NSAID backbone unless a senior disaster or nephrology informed protocol explicitly supports it for that context. The KB position is to hold the NSAID and protect the kidney.

Disaster KBSever and Vanholder 2011

Crush injury is the central reversal in the disaster section. Earthquake and building collapse injuries often involve the limbs, especially the lower limbs. A crushed muscle is injured by pressure while trapped and by reperfusion when blood flow returns. The damaged muscle breaks down and releases its contents into the blood. This process is rhabdomyolysis. The released muscle products can injure the kidneys and cause acute kidney injury.

In most austere settings, NSAIDs are a first line tool. In crush injury, the provider should hold them. The reason is the kidney. Crush injury already threatens renal function, and early aggressive fluids are needed to protect the kidney. NSAIDs can reduce blood flow to the kidney and can cause kidney injury themselves. Giving an NSAID to a patient with suspected crush injury adds harm at the exact site the team is trying to protect.

Regional anesthesia as the elegant disaster solution

For the crushed lower leg, a femoral nerve block or fascia iliaca compartment block fits the disaster logic unusually well.

  • Avoids opioids and therefore avoids respiratory depression in a poorly monitored patient.
  • Avoids NSAIDs and therefore respects the kidney risk in crush injury.
  • Needs little equipment.
  • Can provide hours of relief after one procedure, reducing repeated dosing and provider time.
  • Targets a common disaster pattern: serious lower limb injury.

RAPID study protocol

  • RAPID stands for Regional Anesthesia for Painful Injuries after Disasters.
  • Protocol randomized earthquake victims with serious lower limb injuries in equal numbers to three groups.
  • Group one: standard care with morphine IV or IM at 0.1 mg/kg.
  • Group two: standard care plus landmark guided fascia iliaca compartment block.
  • Group three: standard care plus ultrasound guided femoral nerve block.
  • Blocks used 20 mL of 0.5% levobupivacaine.
  • General humanitarian doctors and nurses performed blocks after short focused training.
  • A companion training study found generalist providers could learn the blocks in a one day course.

Evidence gap

Most regional anesthesia evidence comes from high resource settings, trained proceduralists, and simple single fractures. Disaster patients have complex, multiple, open, contaminated, crush type injuries. Transfer the evidence cautiously.

Disaster KBLevine et al 2016

Regional anesthesia may be the best fit for a common disaster injury. A femoral nerve block or fascia iliaca compartment block can numb much of the lower limb. For the patient with a crushed lower leg, that approach avoids opioids, so it does not threaten breathing in an unmonitored patient. It also avoids NSAIDs, so it spares the kidney that crush injury already threatens. It needs little equipment and can provide hours of relief after one procedure.

The RAPID study protocol was designed around this question. The protocol planned to randomize earthquake victims with serious lower limb injuries to three groups. One group would receive standard care with morphine into a vein or muscle at 0.1 milligrams per kilogram. A second group would receive standard care plus a landmark guided fascia iliaca compartment block. A third group would receive standard care plus an ultrasound guided femoral nerve block. The blocks used 20 milliliters of 0.5% levobupivacaine and were performed by general humanitarian doctors and nurses after focused training. A companion training study found that generalist providers could learn the blocks in a one day course.

The evidence still has a gap. Most support for regional anesthesia comes from high resource settings, skilled proceduralists, and simple single fractures. Disaster patients often have complex, multiple, open, contaminated, crush type injuries. The mechanism and logistics are promising, but disaster specific evidence remains limited.

  • Prolonged casualty care requires plans for repeated reassessment, block wear off, rescue analgesia, and provider fatigue. The KB flags the concept but does not provide a full protocol.

Evidence quality note

The source set states that austere pain care has limited high quality trials. Many recommendations rely on expert consensus and evidence borrowed from hospital, prehospital, military, and global health settings. State what is well supported, what is consensus, and what remains a gap.

The source backbone is the 2024 Wilderness Medical Society acute pain guideline. The 2014 guideline explains the older ladder model. Military TCCC sources provide the triple option model and later prolonged casualty care updates. Mass gathering sources explain event medical system design and the goal of protecting the local health system. Disaster sources support the crush injury reversal and the RAPID regional anesthesia protocol. The altitude illness guideline defines the boundary between headache treatment and altitude illness treatment.

The evidence base is uneven. Some claims are strongly supported. Others are consensus or extrapolated from hospital, prehospital, military, or global health settings. The two clearest gaps are the altitude pharmacokinetic gap and the disaster regional anesthesia gap. The final chapter should name those gaps directly.
